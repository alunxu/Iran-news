  % How does the dominant Western narrative about Iran change---and what remains constant---across five decades of geopolitical upheaval? Drawing on a corpus of 40,381 Iran-related articles from the \textit{New York Times} (1979--2020), extracted via the NYT Archive API, we identify a pattern we term \emph{frame succession}: a sequence of dominant lexical frames---``hostage'' (1979--82, peaking at 37\%), ``military'' (1980s, $\sim$20\%), ``nuclear'' (2003--present, reaching 45\%)---that each displace but never fully erase their predecessors. Throughout, ``diplomacy'' remains marginal at 3--7\%, suggesting that threat-oriented vocabulary systematically crowds out cooperative framing. These findings provide large-scale computational evidence for dynamics that Said theorized qualitatively in \emph{Covering Islam}: the reduction of a complex society to a shifting series of Western security anxieties.

\section{Introduction}
% Edward Said argued in \emph{Covering Islam} \citep{Said1981} that Western journalism does not merely report on the Middle East but actively constructs the categories through which it is perceived. Robert Entman \citep{Entman1993} formalized this intuition: media frames \emph{select} certain aspects of reality and \emph{make them salient}, thereby promoting particular problem definitions and moral evaluations while suppressing alternatives. If Said is right, then the lexical and visual record of a newspaper like the \textit{New York Times} should bear measurable traces of this framing process---not as a static bias, but as a dynamic one that reconfigures itself around each new geopolitical crisis.
The way a nation is perceived is often shaped by how media discourse frames them. For example, Edward Said argued in \emph{Covering Islam} \citep{Said1981} that Western journalism does not merely report on the Middle East but produces the categories through which it is perceived. Robert Entman \citep{Entman1993} formalized this insight: media frames \emph{select} certain aspects of reality and \emph{make them salient}, promoting particular problem definitions while suppressing alternatives. If these claims are correct, the lexical record of a historic and notable newspaper like \textit{The New York Times} (\textit{NYT}) should reveal measurable traces of this process towards various sets of nations and their geopolitical events. 

Particularly, Iran provides a unique case of media framing. Since the 1970s, the country has endured numerous distinct crises ranging from revolution, hostage-taking, proxy warfare, nuclear proliferation, popular protest, to direct military confrontation. It is no secret that Western countries, particularly the United States, have branded Iran as an adversary, and the ongoing 2026 conflict between the U.S. and Iran have only added fuel to this friction. It is this historical relevancy that makes Iran a compelling case study for analyzing the lens of Western media and its ability to frame a nation through reporting. Iranian political discourse itself describes Western media coverage as a form of ``soft war'' (\emph{jang-e narm}), recognizing the power of narrative framing as a geopolitical force \citep{Blout2017}. Thus, examining the NYT’s choice of language, imagery, and analogy may reveal how elite Western journalism frames and interprets international conflict.

% Over the past half-century, the country has repeatedly appeared in Western media narratives through successive geopolitical crises. Since the 1979 Iranian Revolution and the hostage crisis, Iran has been represented through shifting frames associated with regional conflict, nuclear proliferation, sanctions, and political unrest. Events such as debates over the nuclear program and the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) generated extensive international coverage, while more recent developments—including the 2022 “Women, Life, Freedom” protests and rising regional tensions involving Iran, Israel, and the United States—have again placed Iran at the center of global media attention. Examining how these moments are framed in the press provides insight into how Western media constructs and interprets geopolitical crises over time.

Moreover, historical memory plays a significant role in media framing. As Erll \citep{Erll2011} has shown, media narratives continuously rewrite the past in light of the present. For example, a revolution framed as a popular uprising in 1979 may be re-narrated as the origin of state terrorism by 2005. Tracking these renarrations with computational methods provides a systematic way to holistically understand collective memory formation at scale—which is what we hope to attempt by analyzing five decades of the \textit{NYT}’s coverage of Iran.


\section{Dataset}

We use the \textit{New York Times Archive API} \citep{NYTAPI}, which returns structured metadata for every article published since 1851: headline, abstract, lead paragraph, publication date, section, keyword tags (geographic, person, subject, and organization facets), and multimedia metadata with image URLs and captions. While the API does not return full article text, the constraint allows for lower computational overhead during analysis and collection. Additionally, headlines and abstract are typically the most intentionally framed element of newspaper production since they are the components that readers will encounter first \citep{Entman1993}. In short, they are where framing \textit{happens}.

We retrieved monthly archives starting in 1979 (approximately 4 million article records) and filtered for Iran-related content using keyword matching across both structured tags and free-text fields. While the analysis primarily focuses on textual framing, a subset of articles containing images will also be used to explore how visual elements and captions interact with textual narratives.

\textbf{Data adequacy}\quad Table~\ref{tab:periods} shows the resulting corpus of 43,316 articles spans eight distinct geopolitical periods. An initial keyword-prevalence analysis already reveals clear frame-level variation: ``hostage'' dominates at 37\% of Iran headlines during 1979--82, ``nuclear'' rises to 45\% by 2006, ``Protest'' spikes to 20\% in 2022 during the ``Women, Life, Freedom'' movement, and ``Israel'' surges to 49\% in 2024. These confirm that the corpus captures event-driven framing shifts in real time. Throughout all periods, ``diplomacy'' never exceeds 7\%, remaining at 3--4\% even in the most recent years---suggesting that threat-oriented language is highly more prevalent than cooperative language, an asymmetry precisely drawn by Said. Additionally, 9,131 articles (21\%) include image metadata, concentrated from 2004 onward, allowing multimodal analysis of visual--textual framing tensions.

\section{Research Questions}

The patterns we observed motivate three research questions, each examining a different tension within the corpus. In all cases, we analyze both textual framing and the accompanying images (if available)---which can reinforce or contradict the written narrative. For instance, an article framed around diplomacy might be accompanied by images of military parades, protests, or nuclear facilities, introducing visual signals that complicate or intensify the textual framing.

\textbf{RQ1 (Frame discontinuities):} How do Western newspaper representations of Iran change following major geopolitical events? Specifically, do sentiment and topic distributions exhibit statistically measurable breaks around key turning points (1979 Revolution, post-9/11, JCPOA, 2022 ``Women, Life, Freedom'' protests)? If media framing responds to geopolitical shocks, we should observe shifts in vocabulary, sentiment, and dominant topics at these moments.

\textbf{RQ2 (Re-narration):} When the \textit{NYT} references past events in the context of current ones, does the framing vocabulary applied to those past events change? Our co-occurrence analysis offers an initial signal: 381 articles mention both ``revolution'' and ``hostage,'' while 140 link ``revolution'' to ``nuclear''---suggesting that the Revolution is being retroactively absorbed into later threat frames.

\textbf{RQ3 (Genre and thematic framing):} News reporting is conventionally expected to maintain neutrality, while opinion writing often reflects explicit ideological positions. Does the representation of Iran vary systematically between these genres, and across thematic contexts (political coverage, cultural reporting, protest coverage, nuclear program)? Where images are available, we examine whether accompanying visuals reinforce or diverge from the textual frame.

\section{Outlook}

The next phase will apply BERTopic \citep{Grootendorst2022} for dynamic topic modeling, enabling us to track how thematic clusters evolve across decades. Transformer-based sentiment analysis will measure headline-level affect, while structural break detection will identify statistically significant framing shifts at geopolitical turning points. For visual analysis, we plan to annotate a sample of images and cross-tabulate their content categories with textual framing scores. The 2022 ``Women, Life, Freedom'' protests and the 2024--2025 Israel--Iran conflict provide specific test cases: they allow us to examine whether the frame-succession pattern identified in the historical data extends to the present or whether new narrative structures are emerging.
